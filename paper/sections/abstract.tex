\begin{abstract}
Public mouse-brain resources now span complementary levels of organization,
from large-scale electrophysiology and visual prediction benchmarks to local
electron-microscopy and functional coregistration. These resources make partial
digital models of mouse brain increasingly feasible, but they also make it easy
to over-interpret predictive or correlational performance as mechanistic
identifiability. We introduce MouseBrainBench, a reproducible claim-audit
benchmark that separates prediction, reliability, anatomical controls,
structure-function association, and mechanistic identifiability for partial
mouse-brain digital models. The framework integrates synthetic known-truth
cases, Allen Visual Behavior Neuropixels, Sensorium static and dynamic visual
benchmarks, and MICRONS CAVE structure-function data. Across these layers,
MouseBrainBench blocks unsupported claims: Allen provides a reproducible but
mechanistically negative case, Sensorium provides predictive but non-causal
evidence, and Dynamic Sensorium remains a predictive stress test rather than a
mechanistic result. The main positive empirical finding is a replicated MICRONS
local structure-function association: synaptically connected directed pairs show
closer readout-location similarity than distance- and degree-matched
non-connected controls across a discovery cohort and two offset hold-out
cohorts. MouseBrainBench is therefore not a complete mouse-brain simulation; it
is a reproducible audit layer for deciding which claims partial digital models
can and cannot support.
\end{abstract}
