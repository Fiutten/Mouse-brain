\section{Discussion}

MouseBrainBench is useful because it is willing to reject claims. The framework
does not convert every positive correlation into a mechanistic statement.
Instead, it asks what form of evidence is available and what level of
interpretation that evidence supports.

The main positive result is local and specific. In MICRONS CAVE data,
synaptically connected directed pairs are more similar in readout location than
distance- and degree-matched non-connected controls, and this result replicates
across a discovery cohort and two offset hold-outs. This supports a local
structure-function association, not a causal mechanism.

The negative and partial-positive layers are equally important. Allen VBN shows
that reproducibility alone is not sufficient for mechanistic identifiability.
Sensorium static shows that predictive and reliability evidence can be useful
without being causal. Dynamic Sensorium shows that temporal predictive modeling
can improve over simpler baselines while still failing the reliability and
mechanistic gates. Together, these cases demonstrate the value of a claim-audit
framework over a single-score benchmark.

The broader implication is that partial digital models of mouse brain can be
scientifically productive if their claims are bounded. A framework such as
MouseBrainBench can support future integration with larger anatomical,
functional, and simulation resources without implying that any one partial
dataset constitutes a complete digital twin.
