\begin{abstract}
Public mouse-brain resources now permit models to be evaluated against neural
activity, predictive benchmarks, and local synaptic reconstructions. However,
high predictive accuracy is frequently discussed together with biological
fidelity even though prediction, reproducibility, anatomical specificity, and
mechanistic identifiability are different properties. This paper presents
MouseBrainBench, a reproducible framework for auditing the evidence behind
partial digital models of the mouse brain. Its central component is a
Mechanistic Identifiability Score (MIS) that uses non-interchangeable evidence
blocks: reproducibility, topology specificity, and directed identifiability. A
mechanistic claim is admitted only when every required block passes; a high
score in one block cannot compensate for failure in another. The framework is
evaluated through four complementary regimes. Synthetic systems with known
ground truth test the decision rule. Allen Visual Behavior Neuropixels provides
a real negative control, with median cross-mouse and split-half correlations of
0.891 and 0.990 but no topology-specific advantage. Sensorium and Dynamic
Sensorium provide predictive controls in which useful response prediction does
not establish mechanism. MICRONS provides the main structure-function test.
Across a discovery cohort and two non-overlapping hold-outs containing 2991
co-registered units and 5943 connected directed pairs, connected pairs exhibit
greater readout-location similarity than distance- and degree-matched
non-connected pairs. The distance-matched effects are 0.0215, 0.0210, and
0.0179, with positive unit-cluster bootstrap intervals in all cohorts. This
association is compatible with stronger previously published MICRONS findings
and is used as external validation, not as a new biological wiring rule. The
results do not establish causality or a whole-brain digital twin. They show that
MouseBrainBench can distinguish reproducible prediction from a replicated,
local, and anatomically controlled structure-function claim.
\end{abstract}
