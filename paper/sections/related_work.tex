\section{Related work}

\paragraph{Mouse-brain resources.}
The Allen Brain Observatory and Allen Visual Behavior Neuropixels resources
have made standardized large-scale mouse visual-system recordings widely
available for computational reuse \citep{deVries2022AllenSharing}. These
datasets are well suited to evaluating reproducibility and predictive
generalization, but they do not by themselves establish whether a particular
connectivity model is mechanistically identifiable.

\paragraph{Predictive visual benchmarks.}
Sensorium and Dynamic Sensorium provide modern benchmarks for predicting
responses in mouse visual cortex from static images or video stimuli
\citep{sensorium2022,sensorium2023}. They are valuable because they encourage
standardized evaluation and model comparison. In MouseBrainBench, these
datasets are used as predictive and reliability layers, not as evidence of
causal circuit mechanisms.

\paragraph{Connectomics and MICRONS.}
The MICRONS program produced a high-resolution resource linking functional
responses and electron-microscopy structure in mouse visual cortex
\citep{microns2025resource}. This resource is large, but still local rather than
whole brain. MouseBrainBench uses MICRONS as the principal empirical
structure-function benchmark and explicitly blocks extrapolation to whole-brain
digital-twin claims.

\paragraph{Existing simulation and modeling tools.}
Toolkits such as BMTK/SONATA and The Virtual Brain address important simulation
and model-building problems \citep{gratiy2018bmtk,senk2022sonata,tvb2013}.
MouseBrainBench is complementary: it does not replace simulators or define a new
biophysical network format. Instead, it audits what claims are justified by
outputs from data adapters, models, and benchmark analyses.

\paragraph{Mechanistic identifiability.}
Many computational neuroscience workflows emphasize predictive accuracy. The
central premise of MouseBrainBench is that prediction is necessary but not
sufficient for mechanistic interpretation. The benchmark therefore treats
prediction, reproducibility, anatomical specificity, directionality, and
structure-function evidence as separable gates.
