\section{Introduction}

Digital-brain and digital-twin language is becoming increasingly common in
computational neuroscience. At the same time, public mouse-brain resources have
become rich enough to support multi-scale computational modeling: anatomical
atlases, large-scale Neuropixels recordings, visual prediction benchmarks,
mesoscale connectivity resources, and local high-resolution connectomic
datasets are all now available to researchers. This creates an opportunity, but
also a methodological risk. A model can predict a neural response without being
mechanistically identifiable; a dataset can be reproducible without supporting a
specific anatomical mechanism; and a local structure-function result cannot be
expanded into a whole-brain digital-twin claim.

MouseBrainBench addresses this gap. Rather than attempting to reimplement
established simulators or to claim a complete mouse-brain model, it provides a
reproducible benchmark for auditing claims made by partial mouse-brain digital
models. The core idea is deliberately conservative: every model or analysis is
mapped to explicit evidence gates, and only claims supported by the relevant
gates are allowed. Prediction, reliability, topology, directionality,
structure-function association, perturbation behavior, and computational cost
are treated as distinct forms of evidence rather than interchangeable metrics.

The framework is evaluated across several data regimes. Synthetic known-truth
cases test the logic of the Mechanistic Identifiability Score. Allen Visual
Behavior Neuropixels provides a negative mechanistic control: reproducible
signals are detected, but the anatomical and directed-identifiability gates do
not pass. Sensorium static and Dynamic Sensorium provide modern visual
prediction cases, showing that predictive improvements can be useful while still
falling short of causal or mechanistic claims. MICRONS provides the central
positive empirical case: a local structure-function benchmark based on
co-registered functional and electron-microscopy data.

The contribution of this paper is therefore not a mouse-brain digital twin. It
is a reproducible claim-audit framework for partial digital models, together
with a replicated MICRONS structure-function case demonstrating how positive
local evidence can be separated from causal, behavioral, or whole-brain claims.
